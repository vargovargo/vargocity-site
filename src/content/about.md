For twenty-five years I've been tracking a pattern: when large systems get disrupted - the flood maps shift, a pandemic arrives - the people with the fewest resources will have the fewest options. Adaptation is a privilege. My approach has been consistent throughout: measure who benefits and who is harmed, identify root causes, build tools that make the gap legible to people who can act on it. I've applied it to cities, to climate, to wildfire smoke, to COVID, to economic vulnerability. AI looks like the next major disruption on the horizon and it might be the most powerful equalizer we've seen. That's what makes it worth paying attention to.

---

It started with a chemical engineering degree at Michigan — a program that turned out, for me, to be less about thermodynamics than about learning how to learn. Measure things carefully before believing them. Embrace rigor. Check your work. Think in systems. 

I had spent most of my life near a city people were trying to avoid and when I got around to seeing some others I started to recognize what was possible. San Francisco, Saigon, Louisville. Living in new cities showed me why the built environment mattered, how it creates opportunity, and can bring people together.  Studying them showed me how power played out in space: how their potential is distributed. I began to ask who benefits, and who loses. 

That question pulled me south to Atlanta, where I pioneered a dual degree between Georgia Tech's urban planning program and Emory's school of public health. The connection seemed obvious to me: urban planning and public health are both fundamentally about how environments shape human lives. I finished in 2012 with a dissertation on how cities plan (or don't) for extreme heat. In essence, where we build hotter, and who lives there. 

From Atlanta I went to Madison, Wisconsin, as a postdoc working with **Jonathan Patz** at the Global Health Institute and the Nelson Institute for Environmental Studies. Patz's IPCC research helped earn the 2007 Nobel Peace Prize; being in proximity to that kind of ambition clarified what research could be for. **Joel Rogers** — MacArthur Fellow, founder of the Center on Wisconsin Strategy, and the person behind the High Road framework — was across campus. His argument that a thriving economy and a just one aren't trade-offs has shaped how I think about every policy question since. In 2015, I helped found the **UniverCity Alliance** to try to close the gap between what researchers knew and what cities could use. This cross-campus initiative connected UW-Madison's research capacity directly to communities. Since then it's worked with twenty-nine communities across the state. I believe we reach better decisions when we invite more diverse viewpoints into the room, and in practice that means showing up, listening, and making the research useful to the people closest to the problem.

Then I headed west. The California Department of Public Health hired me as lead scientist for its **Climate Change and Health Equity** program. I worked on how wildfire smoke falls disproportionately on communities with lower incomes, limited housing options, and environmental exposures that compound over time. The potential to do better is real, but it requires attention to root causes, not just symptoms.

In early 2020, I was pulled from that work to lead the state's COVID-19 modeling effort. We built a public forecasting dashboard that drew on data ensembles from hundreds of citizen scientists — one of the stranger and more generative collaborative experiences of my career. I developed the state's COVID-19 Health Equity Metric to direct resources toward the communities at greatest risk. Working through all of it with **DJ Patil** — the first U.S. Chief Data Scientist — pressed me to think rigorously about the ethics of every decision: who sees the data, who doesn't, what the model hides, and who gets hurt when it's wrong. I carry that framework into everything since.

Now I'm thinking about how climate risk intersects with economic opportunity in lower-income communities. The framing has shifted from health to economics, but the question is the same: *for whom?*

---

I coined the phrase "metro sapiens" to describe the species we're becoming. More than half of humanity now lives in cities. Within a generation it will be two-thirds. The city is our natural habitat — the environment we've built for ourselves. And like any environment, it can sustain us or fall short of us, depending on how it's designed and for whose benefit.

The goal, in my work and in my life, has been to make healthy choices easy choices. To build environments, whether urban or institutional, where the default option is the good one, and where that default applies equally. I think we're stronger when we share the things we value and are honest with each other about what those things are.
