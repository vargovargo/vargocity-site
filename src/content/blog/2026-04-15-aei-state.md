## AI Task Exposure, State by State
**Independent research · April 2026 · Work in progress**

> This extends the [AEI longitudinal panel](/writing/blog/2026-03-13-aei-longitudinal) to the state level. Findings are descriptive, not causal. Methods are open — see [Data and Code](#data-and-code).

---

## What the State Data Covers

Nationally, Office and Administrative Support accounts for 9.4% of all AI task usage in the March 2026 Anthropic Economic Index release — up from 7.1% in February 2025. That growth matters because Office and Administrative Support is the largest occupation group disproportionately worked by lower-income people, per the Kneebone and Holmes framework this project extends. The national trend is the headline. But national is a mean over a lot of variation.

Three of the five releases we've been tracking include state-level geography. This post extends the panel to that level — 44 to 46 states and territories per release, depending on which cross Anthropic's conversation-count threshold. Most states clear it for only a handful of occupation groups. The full profile of 20-plus major groups shows up in seven: California, New York, Florida, Texas, Washington, Illinois, and Massachusetts. Those are the states where comparisons hold most firmly.

## A Tension in the State Data

The clearest pattern in the overview is a pull between tech concentration and LMI exposure. States where a higher share of AI usage goes to Computer and Mathematical occupations tend to show lower shares in lower-income occupation groups — r ≈ −0.54 across 33 states with sufficient coverage, consistent enough to hold direction through the data.

Washington runs 36% Computer and Mathematical, 11% Office and Administrative Support. Connecticut runs around 30% Computer and Mathematical, 14% Office and Administrative — three to four points above national. Nevada, Utah, Oregon cluster similarly high on the LMI side. The inverse relationship loosely tracks what you'd expect from each state's economic structure: where AI usage has concentrated in the tech sector, it hasn't spread as visibly into the occupation clusters most associated with lower-income work. Where tech concentration is lower, it has.

That isn't an argument that tech states are fine. It's that the question of which workers are encountering AI on the job isn't settled at the national level. Geography is doing real work in the distribution.

---

## How Much of That Pattern Is Real?

About half of it. The r ≈ −0.54 correlation between Computer & Math share and LMI-high share is partly a coverage artifact rather than a geographic signal.

Here is the mechanism. Anthropic only reports data for occupation groups above a conversation-count threshold. Computer and Mathematical is the heaviest-use occupation group — it clears the threshold in nearly every state. Other occupation groups appear only once a state has enough total Claude users for those groups to reach the threshold too. The result: in states where Claude usage is thinner, Computer & Math share is inflated by default. It accounts for a larger slice simply because other groups haven't crossed into the data yet.

Coverage level (the number of occupation groups a state has above threshold) explains about 55% of the variation in Computer & Math share across states. Each additional tracked group is associated with roughly a 1 percentage point drop in Computer & Math share — not because those states have less tech-sector AI use in absolute terms, but because other sectors are now visible in the denominator.

After accounting for coverage, the Computer & Math / LMI-high correlation weakens from r = −0.54 to r = −0.29. Some real geographic pattern remains — states with structurally higher tech-sector AI concentration do show lower LMI-high exposure even after controlling for coverage breadth — but roughly half of the relationship in the scatter is an artifact of how many occupation groups each state has above threshold, which reflects a mix of population size and per-capita Claude usage that the data can't separate.

![Computer and Math vs. Office and Admin share by state coverage level](/plots/state_threshold.png)

Office and Administrative Support behaves differently. Coverage level explains less than 1% of the variance in SOC 43 share across states. The chart makes this visible: Computer & Math share declines sharply as more occupation groups appear; SOC 43 share is essentially flat. Connecticut's 13.8% is 3.9 percentage points above what its coverage level would predict. Louisiana's 6.7% is 3.2 points below. That geographic variation is not a threshold artifact — it appears to reflect genuine differences in where office and administrative AI use is concentrated.

## Office and Administrative Support, State by State

SOC 43 deserves its own attention. It's the largest single LMI-high occupation group, it has the clearest trend line nationally across all five releases, and it's where the state variation is most interpretable.

Connecticut (13.8%), Nevada (12.5%), Utah (12.3%), Colorado (12.2%), Washington (11.4%) all run above the national 9.4%. Louisiana (6.7%), South Carolina (7.0%), Kansas (7.3%) run well below. The above-national cluster spans geographies and economic profiles enough that a single explanation doesn't cover it cleanly. The below-national cluster leans toward states with more agricultural and industrial employment — though the sample sizes in those states require restraint.

![SOC 43 task share by state, V5](/plots/state_soc43_bar.png)

Among the seven states with full enough coverage to anchor comparisons, Florida (10.3%) and Washington (11.4%) both run above national. California (9.9%) and Texas (9.7%) land close to it. New York tracked furthest below in September 2025 — 5.6%, against a national 8.1% — then moved sharply to 9.2% by March 2026.

That convergence is worth watching. Whether it reflects a real shift in which New York workers are using Claude, or an artifact of how threshold dynamics change as total usage grows, this data can't say cleanly. But it's the largest directional move of any state with full coverage across the three-release window we have.

## What the State Data Doesn't Resolve

At the national level, the panel tracks not just which occupations are using AI but how — the share of interactions that are directive (automation-leaning) versus collaborative (augmentation-leaning), broken down by occupation group. That per-occupation breakdown doesn't exist at the state level in the source data. The state-level automation rate is a single number broadcast across all occupation groups in a given state — meaningful as a read on the overall character of AI use there, but not specific enough to say that Office and Administrative Support work in Connecticut is more automating than nationally.

The comparison this visualization is built around — and the one that actually holds — is task share: which occupations account for what portion of AI usage in each state, set against the national distribution.

## Methods

**State task share derivation:** V3 state task shares come from Anthropic's enriched output directly — the `soc_occupation` facet gives pre-computed occupation percentages by state. V4 and V5 state task shares are derived from raw task-level data using the same equal-weight O\*NET crosswalk as the main longitudinal panel: each task distributed proportionally across all major occupation groups it spans, then normalized to 100% within each state.

**State coverage:** States below Anthropic's conversation threshold for a given occupation group don't appear for that group. States with fewer than five occupation groups tracked are shown grayed in the overview — their profiles reflect threshold exclusion, not occupational concentration. The seven states with 15 or more occupation groups tracked (CA, NY, FL, TX, WA, IL, MA) provide the most reliable comparisons.

**Coverage artifact and residual geographic signal:** The number of occupation groups a state has above threshold (n_soc) reflects both population size and per-capita Claude usage — the two cannot be separated with available data. It is used as a proxy for overall conversation volume in that state. In a simple OLS regression, n_soc explains R² = 0.55 of the variance in Computer & Math share and R² = 0.32 of the variance in LMI-high share combined — confirming that the CS/LMI inverse in the scatter is substantially driven by the threshold effect. After partialing n_soc, the Computer & Math / LMI-high correlation falls from r = −0.54 to r = −0.29. By contrast, n_soc explains R² < 0.01 of SOC 43 share variance — the geographic variation in Office & Admin share is orthogonal to coverage level and is treated as genuine in the analysis.

**LMI exposure flags:** Same four-category construction as the main panel — see [Methods in Brief](/writing/blog/2026-03-13-aei-longitudinal#methods-in-brief). This extension is a new construction and should not be attributed to Kneebone and Holmes.

Full derivation decisions are in the [schema comparison appendix](https://github.com/vargovargo/economic-index-trends).
