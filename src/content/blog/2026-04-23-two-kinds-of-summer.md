*Why the common future of more extreme heat means different things in Phoenix and Portland — and why that difference shapes what we build next.*

---

I've spent time in Portland and Phoenix over the past two years for work — two different conferences, both of them about how money and policy can build stronger local economies in lower-income communities. The conferences were mostly indoors. The heat found its way in anyway.

In Portland in 2024, people talked about the heat dome of 2021 the way you talk about a house fire — unprompted, in mid-conversation, about apartments without air conditioning, about the week everyone realized the city wasn't built for this. In Phoenix earlier this year, I had planned to arrive a couple of hours early to hike with a colleague before the conference started. An excessive heat warning was in effect. The trailheads were closed during daylight hours. So we got coffee instead, and talked about the grid, and about whether the cooling-center map still made sense when the cooling season is now eight months long. Two cities, two registers. Same underlying phenomenon.

That difference is the thing I want to unpack here, because I think it matters more than the climate risk conversation usually carries.

Portland's 2021 heat dome was a shock. An event so rare in historical terms that it had effectively no design precedent. Buildings without air conditioning. Emergency rooms without protocols. Outdoor workers without rules. A workforce that knew how to handle snow and rain, not heat at the edge of what the human body can survive. When it arrived, the people who died were disproportionately those with the fewest resources to escape it — older, poorer, alone, in upper-floor apartments without cross-ventilation. Nobody was prepared, and preparation was a luxury unevenly distributed.

Phoenix's record summers are something else. A place that has always been hot, finding that the envelope of what its adaptations can absorb is being exceeded. Cooling infrastructure is everywhere. The grid is enormous. Outdoor work has long been managed by shade, hydration rules, and altered schedules. What's shifting in Phoenix is not the presence of heat but its duration — a heat season stretching into what used to be spring and autumn, nights that no longer cool off, cooling costs climbing into new territory, and adaptations that worked for decades quietly being pushed past the assumptions they were built on. The harm lands on the people already doing work that can't be moved indoors — construction, agriculture, warehousing — and the people in older housing stock where the air conditioning barely keeps up.

These are not the same problem. They call for different investments, different policy, different economic imagination. Most of the climate risk tools we use treat them as one. Your county scores high on heat risk. Fine. What should you build? What should you fund? Who should you protect first? The score doesn't say.

I've spent the past couple of years at the Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco working on the economic implications of extreme heat across the Western states. Some of that work has appeared publicly — [a piece on Phoenix in early 2024](https://www.frbsf.org/research-and-insights/blog/community-development/2024/03/26/extreme-heats-impact-on-a-local-economy-phoenix-arizona/) begins the story. The longer I've sat with the data, the more I've come to think the organizing concept isn't risk level. It's trajectory type. There are at least three shapes of heat trajectory across the West, and each calls for a different response.

The first is **shock**. Places where rare is becoming regular. The Pacific Northwest. Parts of the Intermountain West. Northern California's interior at the margins. These places are hot more often, sometimes dramatically more often, and their infrastructure and institutions are racing to catch up to a hazard they once treated as exceptional.

The second is **stress** — or saturation. Places where familiar is exceeding its envelope. Phoenix. Las Vegas. The Central Valley. Places that have always been hot and are finding that heat seasons are longer, nights are warmer, cooling costs are climbing, and the adaptations that worked for decades are being pushed past their design assumptions.

The third is **shift**. Parts of the northern West where the envelope of viability for various human activities is shifting — north, up in elevation, and not just farming. Growing seasons extending. New crop ranges becoming viable. Outdoor work windows expanding. Recreation economies restructuring around different months. Industrial siting calculations changing. But shift is not gain. Alongside what opens, other things tighten — snowpack thins, water availability declines, fire seasons lengthen, smoke arrives from elsewhere, housing costs compress as new arrivals outbid established residents. What's emerging here isn't opportunity in the frontier sense. It's a different set of constraints meeting a different set of possibilities, and whether that adds up to durable prosperity for the people already living there depends on choices that haven't been made yet.

None of this is democratic. Across the West, the average lower-income tract is projected to face more than twice as many extreme-heat days per year as the wealthiest tracts — a gap driven by fewer trees, more pavement, older housing, and weaker cooling infrastructure, layered on top of the outdoor work that disproportionately falls to lower-income workers. The framing doesn't just tell us what kind of problem a place faces. It tells us who inside that place faces it first.

The climate is changing. How we change with it is the question the typology is actually asking. Shock places need to adapt to the new — to build what wasn't there, because the hazard that arrived wasn't in the design assumptions. Stress places need to adapt the existing — to extend what's there past the envelope it was built for, because what worked for decades won't work for the decades ahead. Shift places need to adapt by moving — to reposition what we do and where we do it, because the places suitable for certain economic activity are no longer the places they used to be. Three shapes of adaptation, three different conversations, three different investment portfolios. Most places contain some of all three, but the weight is usually uneven, and the uneven weight is the thing worth seeing clearly.

Over the next several posts I'll work through what this lens looks like across the domains that shape local economies — labor, health, energy, infrastructure, and agriculture. In each, shock and stress and shift trace different paths, and recognizing which path a place is on is where responding well begins.

Heat is the entry point. The same lens extends to wildfire, flood, and drought, and that is where it begins to intersect with the questions that matter most for whether a place remains somewhere families can actually afford to stay — cost of living, insurance, the quiet collapse of affordability in the places where the risk has outrun the policy.

What I'm working toward is a tool, not a paper. Something here on vargo.city/lab that lets you click a Western county, see the shape of its climate pressure, and read the response portfolio that actually fits it. Posts are the on-ramp. The first one ends here.
