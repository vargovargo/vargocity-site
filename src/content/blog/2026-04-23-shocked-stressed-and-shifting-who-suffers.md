*Adaptation isn't just about geography. It's about who gets protected in the design.*

---

The first post in this series was about the shape of the heat pressure a place faces — shock, stress, or shift. Whether the adaptation a community needs is building something that wasn't there, extending something past its limits, or repositioning what gets done and where. Three shapes of a shared problem.

But averages lie about places.

Inside a county, the heat wave is shared. The suffering isn't. Some people have what they need to get through a heat event intact — air conditioning, physical flexibility, financial margin. Others don't, and the heat translates directly into harm. And when we talk about whether a place is "adapting well" or "falling behind," we are often measuring the experience of the people who were already protected.

This post is about who faces the pressure first. And most.

## The worker at the edge of the envelope

The clearest window into this is what happens on a hot day in a California warehouse.

Jisung Park, Nora Pankratz, and Patrick Behrer analyzed eighteen years of California workers' compensation claims against daily temperature data — the entire universe of injury claims in the nation's largest state workers' comp system from 2001 to 2018. What they found is worth stating plainly. On days in the 90s, workplace injuries rise six to nine percent. On days above 100°F, injuries rise ten to fifteen percent. Across the period, they attribute roughly 20,000 additional workplace injuries per year to heat — somewhere between 260,000 and 450,000 unreported heat-related injuries over eighteen years.

Two things complicate that.

The first is that the effect is not confined to outdoor workers. It shows up in indoor settings — manufacturing, warehousing, wholesale — with the same kind of statistical clarity. A warehouse worker on a hot day is more likely to fall from a ladder, be struck by a vehicle, or mishandle heavy machinery. The injuries coded as falls, collisions, and machinery incidents are, some meaningful fraction of the time, actually heat injuries wearing other names. Cognitive fatigue and dehydration don't show up on the worker's comp claim, but they are part of the injury's cause.

The second is who gets hurt. The effect is about three times larger for men than for women. About twice as large for workers in their twenties and thirties than for workers in their fifties and sixties. And substantially larger at the lower end of the income distribution. The workers the economy can't do without, compensated as though it could, are absorbing most of the hidden cost.

Our 2025 brief on extreme heat in the Western United States found outdoor workers in the region face exposure projected to roughly triple or quadruple by mid-century, with annual wage losses climbing from $1.6 billion historically to somewhere between $5.9 and $12 billion late in the century. Construction, farming, transportation, maintenance — the occupations that can't be moved indoors — are where the hit lands. And across the Western states, the average earnings for eight of eleven heat-vulnerable occupation categories already sit below the regional median. The people most exposed to the physical cost of heat are the people least able to absorb the financial cost of it.

This is the worker-side version of the framework. Heat doesn't fall evenly on a workforce any more than it falls evenly on a region.

## The neighborhood that gets hotter faster

The geography of who gets exposed is finer than counties.

Inside any Western county, some census tracts are substantially hotter than others. Tracts with median household incomes below $60,000 experience an average of nearly thirty days a year with heat index above 100°F. The wealthiest tracts experience about twelve. The gap isn't explained by elevation or latitude — it's explained by tree canopy, paved surface, building age, and the cumulative history of which neighborhoods got shaded and cooled and which ones didn't.

Lower-income households are also more likely to be without air conditioning when the heat arrives, and more likely to live in housing where the air conditioning they do have barely keeps up. Nationally, about fifteen percent of households making under $40,000 lack air conditioning, compared to about seven percent of households making over $100,000. American Indian, Alaska Native, and Pacific Islander households have the highest rates of no-AC housing in the country. When the heat comes, the places that are hottest are the places with the least infrastructure to cool them, full of the people with the least margin to pay for it.

And the cost of staying cool rises faster for the people who can least afford it. A hundred-dollar increase in annual cooling costs is a bigger share of a household making $50,000 than of a household making $100,000. Across communities in the West, the lowest-income census tracts are projected to contribute twice as much of their relative income to cooling cost increases as the highest-income tracts. Utility bills are also the tightest loop in a household's finances — a utility shutoff or a missed rent payment has been shown to push a household into financial precarity faster than an equivalent income loss would. The invisible compounding of heat, utility cost, housing precarity, and missed workdays doesn't show up on anyone's climate risk score. It shows up in emergency room visits, eviction filings, and the next month's rent.

## Adaptation as design choice

Here is what makes all of this genuinely a story about adaptation rather than just exposure.

Park, Pankratz, and Behrer note one finding that cuts against the weight of the rest of their paper. Between 2001 and 2018, the heat-sensitivity of workplace injuries in California declined. The effect of a day above 100°F on worker safety falls by roughly a third over the period. By 2005 onward, days above 100°F no longer produce a statistically distinguishable injury increase at all. 2005 is the year California implemented the first heat safety standard in the country — mandated water, shade, cool-down breaks, and acclimatization protocols for outdoor workers in agriculture, construction, and related sectors.

An adaptation that worked.

Not perfectly, not everywhere, not for every worker — but measurably, over time, in the data, for a specific kind of worker in a specific regulatory environment. Stress adaptation can succeed when the intervention is designed for the exposure. The heat didn't go away; the protection of the people most exposed to it got better.

That's the point I want to sit with. Because the question the framework is really asking — what shape of adaptation does a place need — is also always a question about who the adaptation is *for*. A city can build cooling centers that serve the people who could already get cool on their own. A state can extend its outdoor work windows and leave the most exposed workers in the same conditions they were in before. A region can invest in the grid and let the housing stock where the grid matters most keep degrading. Adaptation is a design choice, and the design choices favor some people and not others.

The tool we are building on this site makes the geography visible. It names the shape of the heat a place faces and suggests a portfolio of responses that fit that shape. But the portfolio only actually changes things if it's designed with the people most exposed in mind. Shock adaptation that doesn't build cooling access for the apartments without air conditioning isn't shock adaptation for the people who died in the 2021 heat dome. Stress adaptation that doesn't extend worker protections across indoor and outdoor sectors isn't stress adaptation for the warehouse worker. Shift adaptation that doesn't plan for in-migration pressure on housing isn't shift adaptation for the people who were already there.

The next post introduces a way to match the shape of a problem to the responses that fit it. A typology that forgets who gets protected is a typology that will describe the problem without changing it.
