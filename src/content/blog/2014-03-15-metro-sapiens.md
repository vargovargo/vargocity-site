## Domesticated Tendencies

John Livingston's (1994) book *The Rogue Primate* posits that humans, not dogs or cats, were the original domesticated animal. A major step in our domestication occurred when we first began to organize and settle in cities. Today, it is easy to take living in cities for granted, but the first cities — established between 4000 and 3000 BC in Mesopotamia — are regarded as an important event in the social evolution of humans. Now, with more than half of the world's population living in cities and all urban development since those first settlements expected to double by 2060, cities are very much the dominant habitat for our species. We are faced with a challenge to resolve our domesticated tendencies with resource and space limits of our planet. A fundamental aim of sustainability is to preserve the existence of our species; it is clear that we will need to embrace urbanism as a human quality to achieve that end.

Today, as a primarily urban species, we are in the age of the *Metro sapiens*. In the same way that "The Anthropocene" has helped define the scale of human impacts within larger contexts of place and time, or how Livingston's domesticated animal argument changes how we see ourselves, reframing cities and human existence solidifies a role for urbanism in sustainability. The idea of Metro sapiens implies that humans are a species evolving to depend on cities. It does not presume that rural lifestyles will cease to exist but, rather, that human populations (in the number currently on Earth) cannot exist by living only rural lifestyles. The corollary is that long-term sustainability will require learning how to support natural systems by living in cities.

Our species' commitment to life in cities demands that we move toward more urbanism, not less. Accomplishing such a goal requires several important steps, all of which expand the fields of environmental and sustainability science and reinforce the value of considering urbanism. First, our relationship with cities should be expected to continue. Second, studies of cities and natural systems must recognize their inherent interdependencies. Finally, the local differences that make some cities healthier and ecologically supportive must be better understood. Each of these steps can generate broad research themes for new discovery. Recognizing our urban nature is a prerequisite.

## Looking Up from 50%

Since the Industrial Revolutions of the eighteenth century, the proportion of humans living in organized settlements has been increasing rapidly. Since 2008, global urbanization has so often been discussed using an obligatory statistic: more than half of the world's population now lives in cities. This depiction of the global urban situation has helped bring urbanism into environmental discussions, but does not capture the true commitment that we, as a species, have to urban lives. It is a snapshot in time when what is needed is a perspective that depicts our urban evolution up to this point and how it can proceed.

We must look up from where we are on the path of urban evolution and see to where we are headed. Is this the end of our urban trajectory or closer to half way? This will help stimulate actions toward sustainability. Focusing only on the 50% threshold can perpetuate the idea that modern dominance over nature is what has brought us this far and what will lead us forward. A benefit of seeing our urban evolution clearly is that we arrive quickly at the important questions of urbanism and sustainability. If the last 300 years of unprecedented movement to cities is coincident with never-before-seen amounts of resource consumption and environmental degradation, then the end result of urbanization as we have been doing is clear, and it appears that what Orr posited in 1979 will continue:

> The ecological basis for modern society is being undermined by the very means that led to its creation.

At the same time, with more than seven billion people on the planet, cities — particularly high-quality urbanism in compact population centers — present the only potential way to accommodate Earth's population sustainably. Whether or not cities can exist without leading to catastrophic environmental degradation is an interesting question that deserves serious attention, thought, and creativity. Whether or not we should be moving to cities is not. The option of urbanism is a false choice that obstructs more useful thought on the topic. Global urbanization is not simply the product of many individual "decisions" to move to cities.

As Metro sapiens, the long-term commitment to living in cities is inherent. This perspective emphasizes that our future is in cities and implies that the survival of an urban species will depend on how we live in cities. This requires capitalizing on our natural inertia toward cities by emphasizing urban advantages that are most supportive of life. Using the 2008 milestone to discuss global urbanization gives us a touchstone for where we, as a species, are on this trajectory but fails to signal its opportunity. Metro sapiens must move toward a richer and permanent relationship of urbanism to environmental sciences. The end result will be improved efforts toward global sustainability.

## Terrestrial Dependencies

Though the phrase is synonymous with a bucolic rural life, "living off the land" is equally applicable to cities. A crucial difference is in scale and reach. Cities are critical for thinking about global sustainability because cities rely on resources well beyond their physical borders. Even within cities, self-sufficient living (think of gardens fed by rain barrels in the backyards of houses covered by solar panels) is commonly mistaken for being sustainable, but in today's globalized world, virtually nothing at a local scale is self-sufficient (think of the building materials for the home or the bikes the residents use to get around). An almost universal characteristic of urbanism is that the resources and materials on which we survive come from places other than where we live. To become meaningful, urban sustainability must address appropriate scales, and these nearly always extend beyond municipal boundaries. Honest pursuit of sustainability must connect our propensity to live urban lives with impacts on the environments that support those lives. Moving forward, there must be more obvious and explicit connections between cities and natural systems.

Our history in cities has thus far fostered a separation from the "natural" world and a dependence upon technological ideas. Some describe this as the condition of the "modern man" free from the bounds of environmental constraint. It fuels a notion that humans thrive by exercising control over the natural environment and assumes an irreversible nature to modernization. This thinking fails to consider our dependence on the natural and undermines the value of biodiversity or quality land, water, soil, and air. Such a perspective does not support the long-term survival of Metro sapiens.

A less severe perspective sees the value of nature, but separates it from the city. Indeed, even in this piece, the parlance of our times lends itself to dramatically distinguishing "urban" things like cities from the natural environment. The environmental and Earth sciences give surprisingly little attention to urban dynamics. Likewise, the language of urban infrastructure has tended to be gray; only recently beginning to think of living ("green") components of the city as infrastructure. The hazards of singling out nature or thinking of wilderness as something separate from humans and cities are discussed thoughtfully in William Cronon's (1996) *The Trouble With Wilderness*. He writes:

> [...] my principal objection to wilderness is that it may teach us to be dismissive or even contemptuous of such humble places and experiences. Without our quite realizing it, wilderness tends to privilege some parts of nature at the expense of others.

Cities are indeed natural and part of nature. They do not function independent of laws of the universe. They are systems engines that constantly transform energy and, in that transformation, require inputs, create waste, and produce disorder. The growth of these systems will not continue by simply optimizing the use of resources or by improving technology. Our existing assumptions about how societies work are continuously at odds with environmental boundaries. New theories must resolve competing paradigms of the "modern" and the "ecological", rather than force us to choose one or the other. The urban-environment system needs to be thought of more holistically.

It is no mistake that as we cross a threshold where the majority of the world is urban, we consume more land, energy, and natural resources than at any point in history. Metro sapiens must reconcile the fact that cities exist because of the natural world, while the existence of cities is degrading that natural world. This, at once, says cities are part of nature and that nature is part of cities. A global urban future depends on developing truly symbiotic relationships between cities and natural environmental systems. There is a need for new theories of urbanization and settlement to incorporate connected, natural environments and vice versa.

## City as Habitat

For Metro sapiens, cities are habitat, and because of the extent to which the inhabitants actively design and construct it, cities are unique in this regard. Like many species, Metro sapiens prosper from a habitat that is healthy and alive. However, unlike other species that may move to find the best habitat, we exert great influence over shaping ours. The places we reside impact our lives and the health of the planet, and it is now widely accepted that the character of settlements influences service delivery, environmental quality, residents' behaviors, and health. Some of the most important questions about urbanization must focus on the character, form, pace, and distribution of urbanization for their impacts on both larger environmental systems and inhabitants.

The built environments that many of us call home tend to encourage resource consumption and unhealthy behaviors. However, as sites of rapid and intentional change, cities present incredible opportunities to improve lives and correct past mistakes. Stephen Kellert (2012) describes the situation in his book *Birthright*:

> [...] four-fifths of the people living in the most developed nations, and for the first time in human history a majority of the world's population, now reside in a city or suburb, generally the most environmentally transformed and degraded of all human environments. [...] Only a deliberate and knowing process of design and development, especially in our urban areas, can restore a world that nurtures and enriches the human body, mind, and spirit through its beneficial association with the natural world.

Kellert's description introduces distinctions in urban form ("cities and suburbs"). As diverse and varied habitats, existing cities help us understand how differences in urban patterns drive environmental change. Describing urban gradients is essential to demonstrating why certain forms of urbanism are less impactful than others. Also, such typologies are crucial for capturing the most important influences of urban habitats on human behavior.

The ways in which place affects our behaviors and health are starting to be more closely examined. Urban design strategies can explicitly focus on prioritizing living systems as parts of urban function. Community design has been linked with physical and mental health outcomes; however, the need for context specificity in design solutions is emphasized. Debates over sprawling and compact development are happening locally, but have importance for regional and global sustainabilities. In cities, projects and individual behaviors understood at the level of the city block can scale up to affect air- and watersheds. Thus, finding ways to relate the actions of neighborhoods and communities with the function of ecoregions, that are the size of nations, will be a considerable challenge for sustainability science.

Urbanism can be defined by quality, diversity, intensity, and connectivity. It should be viewed as a suite of technologies that has changed over time and place and as an ongoing process. The best parts of this suite can be adopted widely and adapted to fit different contexts. Today, scientific fields are beginning to elucidate differences in resource consumption between urban typologies. The prospects of new energy technologies and, thus, larger sustainability efforts vary depending on whether urbanization out to 2060 follows a style of development typified by the sprawling American suburb or one emulating a compact urban center. So too are the health and well-being of individuals connected to differences in their urban habitats. The impacts are local, regional and global, which make cities strategic foci for action toward sustainability.

## Conclusion

As a species, we have a collective self-interest in explicitly considering urbanism as part of environmental and sustainability science. We are at a point in human history where our urban character is clearer than it has ever been before, and it does not appear to be changing. Meaningful progress toward a livable future will require that natural sciences make real efforts to understand and incorporate urbanism. The local benefits of building ourselves better habitats can focus the actions of many individual actors toward larger, common problems. Thus, a future that relies on living in cities is not only a future we are committed to but also our greatest opportunity for genuinely achieving sustainability.

Humans, as Metro sapiens, may be an optimistic vision for the future. It is an alternative to the difficult idea that real constraints on our species may demand far fewer of us on the planet (importantly, city life reinforces lower population growth). The urban perspective is an important way to frame the discussion of our future because it can produce ideas that motivate innovation and cooperation, as opposed to forcing divisions around choices that no one would like to face. Our true evolution is to be seen, which is why this discussion must happen. The sooner, the better.
