Office and Administrative Support is the occupation group most concentrated with lower-income workers among those with high AI exposure — the central finding of Kneebone and Holmes (2025), the Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco working paper this project extends. Thirteen months into tracking the Anthropic Economic Index across five public releases, SOC 43 is also the group with the highest AI task share of any LMI-concentrated occupation: 9.4% of all Claude interactions as of March 2026, the highest it has been across all five releases.

This post covers two things: the updated longitudinal picture for SOC 43 through the V5 release (March 2026), and a refreshed subgroup breakdown — updated for the first time since the [prior post](/lab/posts/2026-03-17-soc43-subgroup) — drilling into the seven O\*NET broad groups that make up SOC 43. The LMI angle is the reason both analyses matter. These are not abstract occupational categories. They are where the people with the least capacity to absorb labor market disruption are doing a disproportionate share of their work.

## The Five-Release Trend

| Release | Task share | Automation rate | Augmentation rate | Task success |
|---------|-----------|----------------|------------------|-------------|
| Feb 2025 (V1) | 7.9% | — | — | — |
| Mar 2025 (V2) | 7.1% | 43.5% | 56.5% | — |
| Sep 2025 (V3) | 8.1% | 62.7% | 37.3% | — |
| Jan 2026 (V4) | 8.3% | 54.3% | 45.7% | 62.8% |
| Mar 2026 (V5) | **9.4%** | 55.8% | 44.2% | 64.9% |

Three things stand out.

**Task share is at its highest point.** The V1 to V2 dip (7.9% to 7.1%) has been more than recovered. SOC 43 now accounts for nearly one in ten Claude interactions, measured by O\*NET task mapping. Whether that trajectory continues is unknown, but there is no sign of it plateauing across the data we have.

**The V3 automation spike now looks like an outlier.** V3 showed SOC 43 automation at 62.7%, a figure that drove significant concern in the prior analysis. V4 (54.3%) and V5 (55.8%) cluster together more than 8 percentage points below that peak. The most plausible reading is that V3 captured a window — August 2025, the week of data collection — where something specific was happening, whether in model behavior following the Claude 4 rollout, in seasonal usage patterns, or in the composition of users active that week. Two consecutive releases at 54–56% does not resolve this but it does reduce the weight we should put on V3 as a reliable anchor.

**Task success in SOC 43 is below the panel average.** The task success metric, introduced in V4 and available in V5, measures the share of interactions Claude assesses as successfully completed. SOC 43's rate in V5 is 64.9%, up from 62.8% in V4 but still below the panel average of roughly 71% across all SOC groups. Among the four LMI-high occupation groups, SOC 43 and Sales (59.2%) are the lowest. The two LMI-high groups with lower AI task share — Healthcare Support (82.7%) and Transportation (74.7%) — have substantially higher success rates. Self-assessed success is a noisy metric and should not be over-interpreted, but the pattern is consistent: the occupations where lower-income workers are most concentrated in AI use are also the occupations where Claude is least confident the interactions are working.

## The Subgroup Picture, Updated Through V5

SOC 43 contains seven O\*NET broad groups ranging from first-line supervisors (43-1) to data entry keyers and word processors (43-9). The prior post established the basic concentration story using V3 and V4. This update adds V5 collaboration data and V5 task share, while being explicit about where the numbers are and are not reliable.

### Task share: 43-9 still dominant, but less so

| Broad group | Title | V4 share | V5 share | V4 tasks | V5 tasks |
|-------------|-------|---------|---------|----------|----------|
| 43-9 | Other Office & Admin Support | 57.6% | **53.2%** | 39 | 40 |
| 43-4 | Information & Record Clerks | 17.3% | 18.7% | 44 | **50** |
| 43-6 | Secretaries & Admin Assistants | 16.1% | 17.6% | 19 | **17** |
| 43-1 | Supervisors | 4.5% | 4.0% | 5 | 6 |
| 43-3 | Financial Clerks | 2.5% | 3.8% | 10 | **15** |
| 43-5 | Material Recording & Dispatching | 1.5% | 2.2% | 6 | **12** |
| 43-2 | Communications Equipment Operators | 0.3% | 0.5% | 2 | 2 |

43-9 dropped from 57.6% to 53.2% of SOC 43's task share between V4 and V5, while 43-4, 43-3, and 43-5 all gained. The basic concentration story holds — 43-9, 43-4, and 43-6 together account for 89% of SOC 43's AI task interactions in V5 — but it is somewhat less extreme than V4 suggested.

### A data quality note that must be read before interpreting V5 subgroup automation rates

Between V4 (January 2026) and V5 (March 2026), the set of O\*NET tasks included in the AEI changed. Several subgroups saw meaningful task count changes:

- **43-4 Information & Record Clerks: +6 tasks** (44 → 50). Tasks added include "answer customers' questions about deposit accounts," "assemble documents for loan closings," "compose correspondence requesting medical information," "provide clients with assistance preparing travel documents," "type acknowledgment letters," and others. Tasks removed include "process, verify, and maintain personnel-related documentation."
- **43-3 Financial Clerks: +5 tasks** (10 → 15). Tasks added include "calculate, prepare, and issue bills and invoices," "compute financial fees and service charges," "operate computers with accounting software," "prepare purchasing files and reports," "review purchase orders and charge slips," and "verify accuracy of billing data."
- **43-5 Material Recording & Dispatching Clerks: +6 tasks** (6 → 12). Tasks added include several customer-facing and logistics-adjacent tasks.
- **43-6 Secretaries & Admin Assistants: −2 tasks** (19 → 17). Tasks removed include "make travel arrangements for executives" and "schedule and make appointments" — both tasks likely to register as augmentation (AI helping a human accomplish a task with ongoing direction) rather than automation.

These are real changes to the task corpus, not matching errors. But they mean that V4-to-V5 changes in automation rates for these subgroups reflect a mixture of two things: genuine change in how Claude is being used, and compositional change from a different task set. The two cannot be separated from the available data.

With that caveat explicitly on the table, here are the three-release automation rates. The interactive chart below shows both task share (V5) and the automation slope across all three releases.

### Automation rates: V3 → V4 → V5

| Broad group | Auto V3 | Auto V4 | Auto V5 | Aug V3 | Aug V4 | Aug V5 |
|-------------|---------|---------|---------|--------|--------|--------|
| 43-9 Other Office & Admin | 41.6% | 29.5% | **29.1%** | 55.0% | 65.7% | 66.2% |
| 43-4 Info & Record Clerks | 46.6% | 40.2% | **46.0%** | 38.8% | 44.5% | 40.6% |
| 43-6 Secretaries & Admin | 56.8% | 42.9% | **47.3%** | 31.7% | 45.9% | 43.0% |
| 43-1 Supervisors | 33.9% | 28.4% | **28.8%** | 56.7% | 62.4% | 62.2% |
| 43-3 Financial Clerks | 41.2% | 38.1% | **35.4%** | 28.4% | 37.5% | 40.5% |
| 43-5 Material Recording | 44.6% | 32.7% | **32.5%** | 28.2% | 30.5% | 36.2% |
| 43-2 Comms Equipment Ops | 39.9% | 37.6% | **35.5%** | 39.4% | 39.4% | 36.2% |

Several patterns survive the task-set caveat because they are consistent across all three releases:

**43-9 (Other Office & Admin Support) shows a sustained augmentation shift.** At 29.1% automation in V5 — down from 41.6% in V3 — this group has moved decisively toward augmentation across every release. This is the subgroup with the most routine and substitutable work in SOC 43 (data entry, word processing, office machine operation), and the direction of the shift is toward AI assisting these workers rather than replacing them end-to-end. That said, the absolute task share of 43-9 (53% of SOC 43) means this pattern has more weight than any other finding in the subgroup analysis.

**43-4 (Information & Record Clerks) remains persistently automation-heavy, but the V5 number is contaminated.** Across V3 (46.6%) and V4 (40.2%), 43-4 was consistently the most automation-oriented large subgroup. The V5 figure of 46.0% represents a jump back toward the V3 level — but 43-4 added 6 tasks between V4 and V5, several of which (billing correspondence, loan document assembly) are plausibly directive rather than collaborative. We cannot determine how much of the 5.8-point increase is real and how much is compositional. The honest read is that 43-4 is and has been the most automation-concentrated subgroup, and V5 does not change that conclusion, but the V4→V5 direction of movement should not be quoted as a clean trend.

**43-6 (Secretaries & Admin Assistants) lost two augmentation-likely tasks between V4 and V5.** The removal of "make travel arrangements for executives" and "schedule and make appointments" from the task set mechanically pushes 43-6's automation rate up, because both removed tasks are the kind of interactive, back-and-forth work that generates augmentation-type interactions. The 4.4-point increase in 43-6's automation rate (42.9% → 47.3%) should be treated with particular skepticism.

**The four small-n subgroups (43-1, 43-3, 43-5, 43-2) collectively represent 8.3% of SOC 43's V5 task share.** Their findings should be treated as indicative rather than reliable across all releases.

## What the LMI Frame Adds

The standard framing for AI labor market analysis asks: which occupations are most exposed? This project asks a different question: among those with high exposure, who is doing that work, and what does the character of the AI engagement look like for them?

The answer in SOC 43 across five releases is:

- The AI task share is high and rising — 9.4% is nearly three times the rate of Healthcare Support (0.3%) and Transportation (0.2%), the other major LMI-high groups.
- The automation rate (55.8% in V5 at the major-group level) is above the global average for Claude AI (45.5%). Among the four LMI-high groups, SOC 43 and Sales both run above global-average automation.
- The task success rate for SOC 43 is below average and below the other LMI-high groups that have lower task share.
- Within SOC 43, the highest task share concentration is in 43-9 — the most routine, substitutable occupations — and that subgroup is shifting toward augmentation.

This combination does not point cleanly in one direction. A rising task share with high automation rates in the subgroups where the work is most routine is a signal worth watching, even as the major-group level shows augmentation still dominant and the task success rate is below average. The picture has gotten more complex with each release, not simpler.

## Methods Notes

**Task share (main panel):** Derived for all 23 SOC major groups using the O\*NET task crosswalk and the `onet_task_pct` facet at US/country geography. Equal-weight distribution when a task spans multiple SOC groups. V5 uses the same derivation as V4. Full methods in [`schema_comparison.md`](https://github.com/vargovargo/economic-index-trends/blob/main/schema_comparison.md).

**Subgroup task share:** V5 task share at the O\*NET broad-group level uses the V5 `onet_task_pct` facet at US/country geography. Tasks deduplicated within broad group before aggregation. Shares normalize to 100% within SOC 43.

**Collaboration rates (subgroup):** Derived from the `onet_task::collaboration` facet at GLOBAL geography — the only geography available for this facet in any release. Task share (US) and collaboration (GLOBAL) are from different geographic filters. This mismatch cannot be corrected without additional data.

**V5 task-set change:** As documented above, the O\*NET task corpus in the AEI changed between V4 and V5. Task counts changed by +1 to +6 across five of the seven SOC 43 broad groups. The V5 collaboration rates for 43-4, 43-3, and 43-5 in particular reflect a different task composition than V4. This project cannot isolate behavioral change from compositional change for these subgroups.

**LMI flag taxonomy:** The `high` LMI flag (SOC groups 31, 41, 43, 53) follows Kneebone and Holmes (2025) directly. The `moderate` and `low` tiers are this project's classification and should not be attributed to K&H.

Full methods: [`soc43_methods.md`](https://github.com/vargovargo/economic-index-trends/blob/main/soc43_methods.md)
