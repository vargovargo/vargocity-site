## The floor, not the poverty line

There's a number most economic reporting doesn't use. It sits on a spectrum.

At one end is the federal poverty line — set so low it describes destitution, not struggle. For a family of three in 2025, that's about $2,100 per month. At the other end are prosperity thresholds that include savings, retirement contributions, and wealth-building. Between them lies a range of sufficiency measures: what researchers call the budgets required to cover basic needs without public assistance and without falling further behind.

The Self-Sufficiency Standard, the MIT Living Wage Calculator, and the EPI Family Budget Calculator all sit in this middle range — above poverty, short of prosperity. They measure related but distinct things. MIT captures basic needs: housing, food, healthcare, transportation, childcare if you have young children. EPI adds a modest-adequacy margin: participation in the community at a basic level, not just survival. The Self-Sufficiency Standard is the most conservative, measuring the income a family needs to meet basic needs without public or private assistance.

In Ada County, Idaho, for a family with one infant, these three sources produce a range of $7,200 to $8,700 per month — roughly 3.5 times the federal poverty line. That gap between what the poverty line says and what sufficiency actually costs is the invisible thing this project is designed to make visible.

This piece reports what the data shows for four western counties: Ada County (Boise) and Canyon County (Nampa) in Idaho, and Clark County (Las Vegas) and Washoe County (Reno) in Nevada.

## What sufficiency actually costs

The chart below shows the monthly cost range for each county, by family structure. The band reflects genuine disagreement between the three anchor sources — they don't agree on the exact number, but they agree on the order of magnitude.

![Monthly sufficiency cost by county and family type](/plots/sbi_monthly_cost.png)

A couple in Boise needs somewhere between $4,900 and $6,300 per month to reach sufficiency. In Las Vegas, $5,300 to $5,700. These aren't luxury budgets. There's no vacation, no savings beyond an emergency fund, no retirement contribution. Just stable.

Add a child under two, and the number jumps by more than $2,000 a month in Idaho.

## The wage gap

Neither state's minimum wage is close.

Idaho's minimum wage is $7.25 per hour — the federal floor, unchanged since 2009. Nevada's is $12.00, raised in 2024 and constitutionally fixed. The question: if both adults in a household work full-time at minimum wage, does their combined income reach the sufficiency floor?

In every county, for every family type, the answer is no.

![Required wage vs. minimum wage by county](/plots/sbi_wage_gap.png)

For a couple in Ada County, Idaho, both adults working full-time at $7.25 need to earn $14.11 per hour to reach the floor — nearly double the minimum. For a family with an infant, that rises to $20.77 per hour. At minimum wage, a single earner covering the full family budget alone would need to work 229 hours a week. There are 168 hours in a week.

Nevada's higher minimum closes some of the gap — the distance between $12.00 and the required wage is smaller — but it doesn't close it. Both adults working full-time at Nevada's minimum still fall short of the sufficiency floor in every county and family configuration measured.

The $4.75 difference between Nevada's and Idaho's minimum wages is the largest policy contrast between neighboring western states in this dataset. It's real money — roughly $9,880 per year for a full-time worker. And it still doesn't clear the bar.

## The cost of having a child

![Cost of a child — monthly floor by household step](/plots/sbi_cost_of_child.png)

In Ada County, going from a couple to a family with one infant adds roughly $2,300 per month to the sufficiency floor. That's the true cost of having a child in Boise — not the incremental cost of diapers and formula, but the full structural cost: infant childcare running $1,000 to $1,100 per month, plus the housing upgrade from a one-bedroom to a two-bedroom unit. Those two items together account for nearly all of the increase.

Canyon County is lower — about $1,900 per month for the same transition — but the structure is identical. Childcare and housing drive it everywhere.

When the child reaches school age, full-time center-based childcare drops off the budget. But other costs rise — school supplies, activities, a third bedroom for two children — and the net monthly cost stays elevated. The school-age family costs more than the couple because the childcare bill is replaced, partially, by other family costs that accumulate as children grow.

The delta between a couple and a new family is the most policy-relevant number in this dataset. It is the direct cost that the childcare system imposes on working families who want to have children. In Idaho, where the minimum wage hasn't moved in 17 years, it is structurally prohibitive.

## Ada and Canyon: same metro, different reality

Ada County (Boise) and Canyon County (Nampa) sit in the same metro area. They share a labor market, an interstate, and the political reality of being the fastest-growing corner of Idaho. They do not share costs.

A family with one infant needs about $7,200 per month to reach sufficiency in Ada County, versus $6,400 in Canyon County — a gap of roughly $810 per month for the same family structure. The primary driver is housing. Ada County's rents, measured by HUD's Small Area Fair Market Rents, run materially higher than Canyon County's despite both counties falling within the same metropolitan statistical area.

Canyon County is often described as the more affordable alternative to Boise. That's true in relative terms. In absolute terms, $6,400 a month is still far beyond what minimum wage provides, and the same structural pressures — rising rents, constrained childcare supply, stagnant wages — apply on both sides of the county line.

## What the proposed minimum wage increase would and wouldn't change

Idaho House Bill 485, pending as of March 2026, would raise Idaho's minimum wage to $12.00 per hour in July 2025, $15.00 in July 2026, and $17.00 in July 2027. It has not passed.

At $12.00 per hour — the bill's first step — no Idaho family configuration in this dataset crosses the sufficiency floor on dual minimum wages. The gap narrows substantially: for a Canyon County couple, the shortfall drops from $5.60 per hour to $0.85 per hour, nearly closing. But for a family with an infant in Ada County, even at $12.00, both adults working full-time fall $8.77 per hour short of the sufficiency floor.

The bill's later steps would get families closer. But the data suggests that for households with young children in high-rent counties, even $17.00 per hour may not fully close the gap.

This isn't an argument for or against the legislation. It's a description of what the data implies about what different wage levels would and wouldn't solve.

## These are 2025 numbers. They're already moving.

Everything reported here is anchored to 2025 dollars using three annual datasets that update once a year. They are rigorous, and they are already dated.

Housing costs in Boise and Reno have moved fast over the past five years. Childcare markets in both states remain undersupplied. Healthcare premiums on the ACA marketplace change each plan year. The numbers in this piece are the best available anchor — they tell you where the floor was in 2025.

A pipeline built alongside this pilot pulls monthly proxy signals — current rents from Zillow, food costs from USDA, fuel prices, healthcare premiums — and tracks them against these annual anchors. The goal is to show how fast the floor is moving, and eventually to compare that movement against headline inflation. Housing, childcare, and healthcare all outpace headline CPI in most years; a sufficiency budget built from those categories rises faster than aggregate inflation statistics suggest. Making that visible requires 12 months of data. The collection is underway. When the comparison is defensible, it will appear here.

## A note on methodology

Three anchor sources underpin every number in this piece: the Self-Sufficiency Standard (UW Pearce Institute), the MIT Living Wage Calculator, and the EPI Family Budget Calculator. They measure related but distinct things — basic needs versus modest adequacy — and they don't always agree. Their disagreement is treated as an uncertainty band rather than averaged away. The floor of every range is the most conservative estimate; the ceiling is the most generous. Full methodology, source descriptions, and data quality notes are in the companion piece: [Building a Survival Budget Index](/writing/blog/2026-03-28-sbi-methods).

The data and code are open: [github.com/vargovargo/cost-of-living](https://github.com/vargovargo/cost-of-living).
